THE BEAUTIFUL RUAHA NATIONAL PARK, TANZANIA

How to get there, animals, accommodation and safari

A few months ago we were asked to make a short film for Paul Tickner Safaris, an amazing safari company who promote wild places using ethical practices . We were to meet Paul and his guests in Ruaha National Park, Tanzania. At this point we were in Lilongwe, Malawi. Flights between African countries are notoriously expensive and with no flights from Lilongwe to Ruaha at a reasonable price (we'd have to go all the way to Dar and then back) we thought we would head by road, at this point Shashe (the old Nissan which had somehow managed the drive from South Africa to Zambia) was dead, so we took the bus. 


We booked our tickets with AXA Bus a few days before, a very reliable bus service in Malawi and took the bus, to Karonga, a town which is famous for being home to a dinosaur, the Malawisaurus. After arriving in Karonga we caught a ride to the Malawi Tanzania border. You can find a shared taxi for around MWK1500 (£1.50 ish) and the taxi drivers wait beside the bus stop. The border crossing was smooth, I guess because we just had backpacks and didn't have to do paperwork for a car, which can take a long time.

Passing over the Songwe river, bodas, mini buses and taxis wait patiently for passengers. The bus to Mbeya comes and goes, so you are always likely to catch one, as seats go, if you are short, you’ll have a good time.The bus takes about 2 to 3 hours to get to Mbeya. We checked in at the Millenium Inn, a very reasonable and comfortable stay over, we were welcomed by a friendly host, a game of football and a towel folded into a swan.

Feeling fresh-ish, we went to catch our bus, we took took the nearest tuk tuk to the bus stop, the sun was slowly showing it’s warm face, creating the most beautiful atmosphere mixing with the smoke from the breakfast fires. The bus journey was quite a distance, I'm not sure the cartographers got it right when they drew the Tanzania map, the distance between Mbeya and Iringa was very very long. So pack a snack, we had a box of cheerios and a lot of grilled corn. We arrived in Iringa, the nearest town to Ruaha National Park, outlined with rocky outcrops that seemed to go on forever.


Once through the gate we headed to Ikuka Lodge. You can use a card to pay your park fees and there are camping sites available which are run by the park, if you are looking for a budget safari option. We had a stop over at Ruaha River (where the National Park gets its name) and stretched our legs on the bridge. The drive to the lodge was incredible, rolling hills, majestic open plains, fat baobabs and a handsome elephant eating his lunch. Ruaha is home to the largest elephant population in Tanzania with 12,000 elephants migrating through the greater Ruaha each year. Far on the hill we could see our lodging for the next few days nearing...
